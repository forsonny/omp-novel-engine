# AI-ism Detection Rubric for Fiction

This rubric is novel-agnostic. Apply it to any genre, point of view, tense, or style.

AI-ism does not mean proof of AI authorship. It means the prose displays patterns that can make readers suspect machine assistance because the text feels over-managed, too coherent, too polished, emotionally pre-interpreted, or distant from character experience.


## Reporting requirement

For full chapter reviews, these categories are not sampling buckets. They are inventory buckets.

List every occurrence you find. If the same category appears ten times, report ten occurrences or group them in a table with ten quoted entries. Do not provide one example and imply the category has been covered.

Use `OCCURRENCE_INVENTORY_REPORTING.md` as the controlling rule for report completeness.

## Risk scale

### Low

The prose feels natural. It may contain some polish or symbolism, but the scene trusts the reader and remains grounded in character experience.

### Low-medium

A few patterns appear, but they do not dominate the prose. Minor trimming or local revision is enough.

### Medium

Several patterns repeat. The prose may feel over-shaped, too lyrical in a uniform way, or too eager to explain its meaning.

### Medium-high

The prose repeatedly announces significance, explains emotions, converts objects into symbols, or uses narrator-distance constructions. Revision should be systematic.

### High

The prose feels mechanically generated, with frequent over-explanation, uniform rhythm, clean moral conclusions, repeated tics, and characters functioning as thematic devices rather than people.

## Flexible risk patterns

These patterns are not always wrong. They become AI-ism when they are frequent, redundant, or used in place of direct experience.

## 1. Theme over-reinforcement

The same motif, symbol, emotional idea, or lesson appears repeatedly in a scene.

Flag when the text keeps reminding the reader what the scene means.

Common signs:

- the same symbol returns with the same meaning
- the narration names the theme several times
- multiple metaphors express the same emotional idea
- the ending repeats what the scene already showed

Fix:

- keep the strongest expression
- remove duplicate explanations
- let the reader infer meaning
- move from abstraction back into action or perception

## 2. Emotional over-explanation

The prose explains emotions already visible through behavior, dialogue, silence, or context.

Common signs:

- character self-diagnosis
- narrator explains why a gesture matters
- emotion is translated into a lesson
- the scene pauses to summarize a relationship

Fix:

- replace explanation with gesture
- replace summary with specific memory
- let silence, mistake, avoidance, or physical behavior carry emotion

## 3. Symbolic conversion

A concrete event, object, or action immediately becomes abstract meaning.

Common signs:

- an object is interpreted the moment it appears
- a physical event becomes a life lesson
- imagery is followed by a statement of what it symbolizes

Fix:

- keep the object concrete
- give the object history or sensory detail
- delay interpretation
- allow meaning to accumulate

## 4. Excessive fragment emphasis

The prose relies too often on one-line paragraphs, isolated words, and sentence fragments for drama.

Common signs:

- many standalone fragments in a row
- every emotional beat gets its own paragraph
- tension comes from formatting rather than event pressure

Fix:

- keep only the strongest fragments
- merge weaker fragments
- vary sentence length
- use fragments when thought actually breaks

## 5. Excessive symmetry

Characters, scenes, images, or dialogue function as overly clean opposites.

Common signs:

- one character always acts, another always hesitates
- one character is all instinct, another all logic
- repeated mirrors and oppositions
- character roles feel designed around theme

Fix:

- add contradiction
- add non-thematic behavior
- let characters violate their pattern
- give each person private motives beyond the theme

## 6. Narrative self-awareness

The story explains its own arc, message, or meaning.

Common signs:

- thesis-like sentences
- chapter endings that state the lesson
- direct definitions of courage, grief, love, power, identity, or truth
- lines that sound designed for quotation

Fix:

- replace thesis with action
- end on image, gesture, dialogue, choice, or unresolved detail
- trust the reader

## 7. Uniform beauty

Every sentence sounds polished, profound, lyrical, or emotionally weighted.

Common signs:

- little variation between plain and poetic language
- every line reaches for beauty
- every image feels curated
- physical mess is cleaned into literary meaning

Fix:

- add plain sentences
- add rough physical detail
- allow awkward thoughts
- save lyricism for earned moments

## 8. Complete realization too early

A character fully understands their flaw, wound, lesson, or future path inside one scene.

Common signs:

- deathbed clarity
- sudden full self-knowledge
- clean emotional breakthrough
- ending that explains who the character must become

Fix:

- leave realization partial
- preserve confusion
- use choices instead of self-analysis
- stretch growth across scenes

## 9. Over-clean cause and effect

The prose explains why a character behaves a certain way with too much psychological neatness.

Common signs:

- a reaction is followed by a complete backstory explanation
- motives are named instead of dramatized
- trauma is used as an immediate key to unlock behavior

Fix:

- show evidence
- let motives remain partly hidden
- use repeated behavior or objects
- avoid turning psychology into summary

## 10. Repeated profound closing lines

Scenes end with polished thematic sentences too often.

Common signs:

- endings sound like quotes
- grief, love, fear, courage, or identity is defined
- the final sentence explains the emotional meaning of the scene

Fix:

- end on action, image, mistake, or unresolved dialogue
- avoid summing up the lesson
- let the scene echo instead of conclude

## Hard narrator-distance bans

These patterns are stronger than flexible risks. In narration, they should be removed unless a listed exception applies.

Full rules are in `NARRATOR_DISTANCE_BANS.md`.

Summary:

1. Ban "the way" comparisons in narration.
2. Ban type-casting phrases such as "the calm of a person who..."
3. Ban negative definition chains such as "Not angry. Not afraid. Empty."
4. Ban retrospective framing such as "I did not know yet" or "Later, I would learn."
5. Ban significance announcements such as "Something had changed."
6. Ban emotional analysis immediately after emotional observation.

## Master revision principle

Do not make the prose bland.

The goal is controlled imperfection: rhythm, texture, implication, contradiction, and enough roughness to feel observed instead of optimized.

# Plain Physical Prose Risk Patterns

These patterns overlap with AI-ism but deserve their own pass because they are often the fastest way to make prose feel more natural.

## 11. Decorative verbs

Inanimate things are given dramatic human or animal actions.

Common signs:

- engines cough or groan
- cities breathe
- silence presses
- doors whisper
- water tears
- light spills with intention

Fix:

Use the real physical action: revved, idled, flapped, rushed, shut, moved, hit, fell, slid.

## 12. Poetic scene endings

The last line tries to summarize the emotional meaning of the scene.

Common signs:

- a line designed to land hard
- a final sentence that explains the image
- quote-like grief, courage, loss, or change lines

Fix:

End on the last physical fact. Stop before the explanation.

## 13. Implausibly beautiful observation

A character under stress notices a polished visual image that does not match their focus.

Common signs:

- light quality during drowning, running, fighting, or panic
- elegant arrangement of the scene during immediate danger
- imagery that sounds like the narrator, not the character

Fix:

Replace beauty with functional perception. What does the character need to know right now?

## 14. Redundant explanatory sentence

A strong sentence is followed by another sentence that explains it.

Common signs:

- second sentence can begin with "In other words"
- the action already proves the meaning
- silence, laughter, fear, or breakage is explained immediately after it appears

Fix:

Delete the second sentence.

## 15. Cushioning around hard facts

The prose prepares the reader for a hard event or softens it after it happens.

Common signs:

- "Before she could react"
- "He found himself unable"
- "The realization hit him"
- "He watched as"

Fix:

Write the hard fact directly.
