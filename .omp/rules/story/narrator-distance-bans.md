# Narrator-Distance Bans

These rules target sentence constructions that place the narrator between the reader and the character's direct experience.

They are called narrator-distance bans because the problem is not simply style. The problem is that the prose stops perceiving and starts explaining perception.

These bans apply to narration. Dialogue has more freedom because characters naturally speak in comparisons, negations, and interpretive phrasing.

## Master rule

In narration, direct perception beats narrated interpretation.

Prefer:

- what the character sees
- what the character hears
- what the character does
- what changes in the room
- what another character says
- what the body registers

Avoid:

- narrator comparison
- narrator type-casting
- narrator preview of later knowledge
- narrator announcements of significance
- narrator analysis of emotion immediately after observation

## Ban 1: "The way" comparisons in narration

### Banned construction

- X the way Y
- the same way
- the way you would
- the way a person would
- the way a child would
- the way a soldier would
- the way someone would

### Why it creates AI-ism

This construction often replaces direct perception with analogy. It can sound vivid once, but repeated use makes the prose feel like it is explaining experience instead of rendering it.

### Bad example

He watched her the way a hunter watches a treeline.

### Better example

He watched her hands first. Then the door. Then the knife on the table.

### Bad example

The crowd shifted the same way water moves around a stone.

### Better example

The people nearest him stepped aside. The ones behind them followed a beat later, glancing over shoulders they pretended not to turn.

### Rule

Do not use this construction in narration. Find the direct observation.

### Exception

Dialogue may use it when it fits voice.

Example:

"You fight the way a drunk kicks a door."

## Ban 2: Type-casting with "the quality of a person who"

### Banned construction

Phrases that describe someone by assigning them a behavioral type.

Common forms:

- the confidence of a man who had done this before
- the calm of someone who knew he had already won
- the expression of a woman who had decided not to care
- the precision of a person who considered urgency beneath him
- the patience of a teacher waiting for a slow student

### Why it creates AI-ism

The narrator gives a character profile instead of an observation. The prose tells the reader what kind of person this is instead of letting behavior prove it.

### Bad example

She spoke with the quiet confidence of a woman who had done this many times before.

### Better example

She did not raise her voice. No one interrupted her.

### Bad example

He moved with the patience of a man who knew the room belonged to him.

### Better example

He took off his gloves finger by finger. Everyone waited.

### Rule

Do not type-cast in narration. Write the observable detail.

### Exception

None. This construction should be rewritten.

## Ban 3: Negative definition chains

### Banned construction

A chain of two or more negated labels used to arrive at a positive meaning.

Common forms:

- Not angry. Not afraid. Empty.
- Not grief. Not guilt. Something worse.
- He was not smiling, not frowning, not anything.
- She did not look hurt. She did not look surprised. She looked finished.

### Why it creates AI-ism

The narrator circles the meaning by listing rejected options. This can feel like the prose is thinking through categories instead of perceiving.

### Bad example

Not angry. Not amused. Assessing.

### Better example

His eyes moved from my face to my hands and stayed there.

### Bad example

She was not sad, not frightened, not ashamed. She was gone from herself.

### Better example

She stared past him. When he said her name, she blinked once and kept staring.

### Rule

Do not define emotion or state through a chain of negations.

### Exceptions

A single negation can be fine when it provides concrete information.

Example:

He did not flinch.

Dialogue may use negation chains if it fits character voice.

Example:

"I am not angry. I am not disappointed. I am done."

## Ban 4: Retrospective narrator framing

### Banned construction

The narrator references future knowledge from inside a scene.

Common forms:

- I did not know yet
- Later, I would learn
- I would not understand until years later
- I did not know what that meant
- which I would later discover
- I had no way of knowing
- I would remember that moment forever

### Why it creates AI-ism

The prose leaks outline knowledge. The character is supposed to be experiencing the scene, but the narrator steps into a future position and comments on it.

### Bad example

I did not know yet that speaking to the spirit out loud was forbidden.

### Better example

The spirit spoke. I answered. The room went quiet.

### Bad example

Later, I would learn that the mark on his wrist belonged to the royal guard.

### Better example

A black mark crossed his wrist. When his sleeve slipped, the nearest servant looked away.

### Rule

If the character does not know something in the scene, do not reference the gap from a future position. Let consequences arrive through events.

### Exception

A deliberately framed retrospective narrator may use this if the framing device is established early and maintained consistently.

## Ban 5: Significance announcements

### Banned construction

Sentences that announce a shift, realization, or importance before showing it.

Common forms:

- Something had changed.
- Everything was different now.
- That was when I realized.
- I understood then.
- The room had shifted.
- Nothing would ever be the same.
- I could feel it in the air.
- A line had been crossed.

### Why it creates AI-ism

The narrator points at the moment and says it matters. This weakens the moment because the prose announces instead of enacts.

### Bad example

Something had changed. I could feel it in the room.

### Better example

The captain stopped smiling. The two guards behind him moved their hands to their sword belts.

### Bad example

That was when I realized he was afraid of me.

### Better example

He stepped back before I finished speaking.

### Rule

Do not announce the change. Show the changed behavior, changed space, changed stakes, or changed physical response.

### Exception

None. Replace the announcement with the specific observation.

## Ban 6: Emotional analysis after observation

### Banned construction

An emotional observation is immediately followed by analysis of the emotion's pattern, origin, or meaning.

### Why it creates AI-ism

The prose does not trust the observation. It pauses to explain what the reader should understand.

### Bad example

She said it lightly. I had noticed that about her, how she hid fear under warmth whenever the truth got too close.

### Better example

She said it lightly. I did not push.

### Bad example

He laughed too quickly. That was his habit when shame caught up to him.

### Better example

He laughed too quickly and reached for the cup. His hand missed the handle.

### Rule

After an emotional observation, move to action, dialogue, physical detail, or new perception. Do not analyze what was just observed.

### Exception

In an interior-monologue scene where emotional processing is the event of the scene, analysis can be appropriate. Use sparingly.

## Quick rewrite recipes

### Comparison to perception

Banned:

He looked at me the way a judge looks at a guilty man.

Direct:

He looked at the blood on my sleeve, then at my face. He did not ask whose it was.

### Type-cast to behavior

Banned:

She had the calm of someone who had already won.

Direct:

She sat while the rest of us stood.

### Negative chain to evidence

Banned:

Not fear. Not anger. Recognition.

Direct:

His eyes widened at the scar on my wrist.

### Retrospective frame to present consequence

Banned:

I did not know yet that the symbol meant exile.

Direct:

The innkeeper saw the symbol and pushed my coins back across the counter.

### Significance announcement to changed behavior

Banned:

Everything shifted.

Direct:

The musicians stopped playing.

### Emotional analysis to next action

Banned:

He smiled because he did not know how to ask for help.

Direct:

He smiled. The bandage slipped through his fingers and fell to the floor.

## Master scan

After drafting any passage, search narration for:

1. "the way"
2. "the same way"
3. "of someone who"
4. "of a man who"
5. "of a woman who"
6. chains of two or more "not" definitions
7. "I did not know yet"
8. "Later, I would"
9. "I would learn"
10. "Something had changed"
11. "That was when"
12. "I realized"
13. "Everything was different"
14. emotional observation followed by explanation

If found in narration, rewrite directly.
