# Plain Physical Prose Pass

This pass is for prose that feels like it is trying too hard to be good.

The core problem: AI prose often tries to sound literary. It reaches for interesting verbs, elegant phrases, poetic endings, clever observations, and emotional summary. A strong natural writer usually does less. They say what happened, choose concrete facts, and let the reader feel the pressure.

Before rewriting anything, read the whole chapter once and ask: where is this trying too hard?

This pass is not a command to make prose bland. It is a command to replace decorative writing with precise physical writing.

## What this pass protects

Keep:

- story facts
- scene order
- character intent
- dialogue
- unusual character habits that reveal personality
- plainness
- short sentences
- simple verbs
- direct physical facts

Do not cut something just because it is unusual. Cut it when it exists mainly to sound good.

## The seven things to fix

### 1. Decorative verbs

AI gives inanimate things human or animal actions to make the writing feel alive.

Look for verbs where the subject cannot literally perform the action.

Examples to flag:

- A bus coughed behind the depot wall.
- The bus engine groaned.
- The orange sticker slapped in the rain.
- Water tore around the support.
- The city breathed around her.
- The silence pressed in.
- The door whispered shut.

Better alternatives:

- A bus revved behind the depot wall.
- The bus engine revved.
- The orange sticker flapped in the rain.
- Water rushed around the support.
- Traffic moved around her.
- No one spoke.
- The door shut.

Fix rule:

Replace the decorative verb with the real physical action. If there is no clear physical action, cut the sentence.

Test:

Could this verb appear in a news report without sounding strange? If not, it is probably decorative.

### 2. Poetic endings to scenes and chapters

AI often ends a scene with a line that tries to land hard. It usually summarizes the emotional meaning of what just happened.

Flag endings that sound like quotes, thesis statements, or emotional captions.

Examples to flag:

- One step, he thought. The rest of it came apart before the words could find an end.
- Cold closed over Jonah before fear could finish forming.
- Whatever came next, she was ready.
- The word vanished in rain.
- Eli's hand stayed there after there was nothing left to reach for.

Better alternatives:

- The cold hit him before he had time to be scared.
- She picked up the bag and opened the door.
- He said nothing.
- Eli's hand was still at the fence.

Fix rule:

End on the last physical fact. Do not add a clause that explains what the fact means.

### 3. Beautiful observations a person would not make

AI often gives characters elegant perceptions that do not match their situation.

A character who is drowning, running, fighting, hiding, bleeding, or terrified usually notices what affects survival, movement, pain, and direction. They usually do not notice beautiful light quality, elegant symbolic arrangements, or curated imagery.

Examples to flag:

- the rain around his shoulders looked gold
- a smear of gold light
- the shelter light broke into pieces
- the city glittered beneath the fog
- the moon laid silver over the battlefield while arrows fell

Better alternatives:

- the bus lights were on behind him
- a blur of light above him
- the light got farther away
- the street was wet
- arrows hit the dirt near his feet

Fix rule:

Ask what the character would actually perceive given their stress, focus, and immediate need. Replace beauty with function.

### 4. Over-explained emotion

AI names a feeling and then explains it.

Examples to flag:

- He felt a wave of grief wash over him as he remembered how close they had once been.
- She was scared, more scared than she had ever been in her life.
- He was angry, the kind of anger that comes from years of being ignored.

Better alternatives:

- He put the photo face down.
- She backed into the table and did not look away.
- He closed his hand around the glass until his knuckles went pale.

Fix rule:

Cut everything after the emotion tag. Then consider cutting the emotion tag too. Show what the character does with hands, face, voice, breath, movement, silence, or objects.

### 5. Sentences that explain what the previous sentence already showed

AI often follows a strong sentence with a second sentence that explains it.

Examples to flag:

- The board broke. It could no longer hold his weight.
- Jonah laughed. It was not the laugh of someone who found anything funny.
- She did not answer. The silence said everything.

Better alternatives:

- The board broke.
- Jonah laughed. It came out small and wrong.
- She did not answer.

Fix rule:

Delete the explaining sentence.

Test:

If the second sentence could begin with "In other words," delete it.

### 6. Cushioning around hard facts

AI often softens hard events by preparing the reader before them or interpreting them afterward.

Examples to flag:

- Before she could react, the car hit her.
- He found himself unable to stop the tears that came.
- The realization hit him that he might not make it out.

Better alternatives:

- The car hit her.
- He cried.
- He might not make it out.

Fix rule:

Find the hard fact. Write it. Move to the next thing. Do not prepare the reader. Do not help the reader process it after.

### 7. Prose that notices its own cleverness

AI sometimes writes a striking line and then adds another sentence that makes the striking line feel significant.

Example to flag:

- "Stupid," he said. The word vanished in rain.

Better alternative:

- "Stupid," he said.

Fix rule:

If a sentence exists to make the previous sentence feel more meaningful, delete it.

## Rewrite workflow

### Step 1: Read the whole chapter first

Do not start fixing line by line. First understand what happens, what the reader needs to know, and what the characters are doing.

### Step 2: Mark all seven problem types

Find the problems before revising. Mark decorative verbs, poetic endings, impossible observations, over-explained emotion, redundant explanation, cushioning, and cleverness signals.

### Step 3: Rewrite without changing story facts

Keep the same events, order, characters, and dialogue unless the user asks for deeper revision.

Use shorter sentences and simpler words when possible.

Prefer:

- He ran.
- She looked at him.
- The water was cold.
- The bridge shook.
- The door shut.

Avoid:

- He sprinted, driven by panic.
- Her eyes found his.
- The cold hit him like a wall.
- The bridge groaned beneath the storm.
- The door whispered shut.

### Step 4: Check the rewrite for new AI-ism

AI that tries to fix AI writing often replaces one decorative phrase with another. Read the revised passage again and remove any new decorative verbs, poetic phrases, or explanatory sentences.

## One-sentence test

After revision, read each sentence and ask: would a person actually write this, or does it sound like writing trying to be writing?

Person version:

- He got to Iron Street just as the rain turned the gutter water brown.

AI-ish version:

- As Jonah Vale arrived at Iron Street, the relentless downpour had transformed the once-clear gutter water into a murky brown torrent.

The person version uses plain words in plain order. The AI-ish version stacks adjectives, adds unnecessary contrast, and decorates the rain.

When a sentence sounds like it is trying to be writing, rewrite it until it just says what happened.
