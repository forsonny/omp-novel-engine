# Quick Scan Patterns

Use this as a practical checklist.

- the way

## Pattern checks that require judgment

### Negative definition chain

Look for two or more negations in a row used to define an emotion or state.

Examples:

- Not angry. Not afraid. Empty.
- It was not grief, not guilt, but something colder.
- She was not crying, not shaking, not pleading.

Single negations can be fine.

### Emotional analysis after observation

Look for this sequence:

1. A character shows an emotion.
2. The narration explains the character's emotional habit, motive, origin, or pattern.
3. The scene pauses.

Rewrite by moving to action, dialogue, or new perception.

### Repeated fragment emphasis

Scan for clusters of one-word or two-word paragraphs.

Ask whether each fragment earns its isolation.

### Profound closing line

Check the final paragraph of each scene.

If it defines the meaning of the scene, revise toward image, consequence, or action.

## Safe alternatives

Use:

- direct physical detail
- character action
- changed behavior in the room
- sensory interruption
- specific object
- incomplete thought
- dialogue with subtext
- silence
- consequence
- contradiction

## Review note

When reporting to the user, do not just say "remove this." Explain what to replace it with.

# Quick Scan: Plain Physical Prose

## Decorative verb scan

Look for inanimate subjects performing human, animal, or emotionally loaded actions.

Common subject plus verb risks:

- bus coughed
- engine groaned
- sticker slapped
- water tore
- city breathed
- silence pressed
- door whispered
- light danced
- shadows crawled
- wind screamed

Not every vivid verb is wrong, but if the sentence sounds like it wants to be literary, replace it with physical action.

## Poetic ending scan

Check the final paragraph of each scene or chapter.

Flag if the ending:

- explains the image
- states the meaning
- sounds quote-ready
- adds emotional interpretation after the last action

## Previous-sentence explanation scan

Read sentence pairs.

If sentence two could begin with "In other words," delete it.

## Stress perception scan

For danger scenes, ask:

- What does the character need?
- What hurts?
- Where can they move?
- What do they see that affects survival?

Cut decorative light, color, and symbolic arrangement unless the character has a believable reason to notice it.

## Hard fact scan

Search for cushioning phrases:

- before she could react
- before he could stop himself
- he found himself unable
- he watched as
- the realization hit him
- he became aware that

Rewrite to the fact.


# Exhaustive Reporting Checklist

Use this checklist before producing any full review.

## Must include

- Count summary table
- Exhaustive occurrence inventory table
- Every hard-ban hit
- Every plain physical prose issue found
- Every flexible AI-ism issue that materially weakens naturalness
- Minor or near-miss findings, either in the main table or appendix

## Do not do this

- Do not give one quote per category and move on.
- Do not say "examples include" unless the user asked for a brief review.
- Do not say "mostly clean" without showing what was scanned.
- Do not hide low-risk findings inside a general paragraph.
- Do not invent line numbers.

## Occurrence inventory fields

For every finding, record:

1. Primary category
2. Severity
3. Exact quote or location
4. Why it is a problem
5. Fix strategy
6. Optional secondary tag

## Final self-check

Before answering, ask:

- Did I list every occurrence I found?
- Did I accidentally use one representative example instead of the full list?
- Did I say a category has a problem without listing all the quotes?
- Did I include counts?
- Did I mark clean categories as none found?

If any answer is no, revise the report before sending it.
