# Revision Method for Reducing AI-ism

Use this method when revising prose.

## Step 1: Identify the scene's real job

Before changing anything, decide what the passage needs to accomplish.

Possible jobs:

- create danger
- reveal character
- intensify grief
- show attraction
- establish power imbalance
- dramatize a decision
- create wonder
- build dread
- transition into a new world
- resolve a conflict
- complicate a relationship

Do not cut prose that serves the scene's job.

## Step 2: Separate strength from excess

For each strong passage, ask:

- Is this line powerful because it reveals something?
- Or is it repeating what the scene already showed?

Keep the first. Cut or reduce the second.

## Step 3: Run the flexible AI-ism pass

Check for:

- repeated theme language
- over-explained emotion
- symbolic conversion
- excessive fragments
- too-clean oppositions
- thesis-like narration
- uniform beauty
- complete realization too early
- over-clean psychological explanation
- profound closing lines

Mark every occurrence that materially affects naturalness. Do not mark one example per category and stop.

## Step 4: Run the hard narrator-distance ban scan

Search narration for:

- "the way"
- "the same way"
- "the way you would"
- "of someone who"
- "of a man who"
- "of a woman who"
- "I did not know yet"
- "Later, I would"
- "I would learn"
- "Something had changed"
- "That was when"
- "I realized"
- repeated "Not X. Not Y."
- emotional observation followed by analysis

Rewrite these unless a valid exception applies.

## Step 5: Build the occurrence inventory

Before reporting or rewriting, create an inventory of every flagged issue. Include the quote, category, severity, and fix strategy. Use this inventory to drive the report.

For review mode, show the inventory to the user. For revision mode, use the inventory internally unless the user asks to see it.

## Step 6: Replace explanation with evidence

Use this conversion table.

### Theme statement to action

Before:

She understood that trust was the only bridge left between them.

After:

She gave him the key and did not close her hand until he took it.

### Emotion diagnosis to behavior

Before:

He was afraid of being abandoned.

After:

He packed before anyone asked him to leave.

### Symbol explanation to physical detail

Before:

The cracked mirror showed the broken person she had become.

After:

The mirror split her face at the mouth. She wiped steam from the glass and looked away.

### Comparison to perception

Before:

He watched the room the way a wolf watches a trap.

After:

His gaze moved from the windows to the latch, then to the space under the table.

### Significance announcement to changed behavior

Before:

Everything changed after that.

After:

No one used his title when he returned to the table.

## Step 7: Add controlled imperfection

Natural prose often has variation.

Add:

- plain lines after lyrical ones
- physical discomfort
- incomplete thoughts
- small mistakes
- sensory interruptions
- awkward gestures
- contradictory behavior
- character-specific habits

Do not add random mess. Add meaningful texture.

## Step 8: Preserve genre fit

Different genres tolerate different levels of intensity.

### Literary fiction

Can carry more implication, silence, and ambiguity.

### Fantasy and progression fantasy

Can carry more heightened language, awe, and mythic framing, but still needs direct perception.

### Thriller

Needs speed, clarity, and concrete stakes.

### Romance

Needs body language, subtext, and emotional specificity without constant explanation.

### Horror

Needs sensory dread and uncertainty. Do not over-explain the fear.

### Young adult

Can use direct emotional language, but should still avoid narrator-distance tics and over-complete realizations.

## Step 9: Check the ending

Scene endings are high-risk.

Avoid ending every scene with:

- a lesson
- a definition
- a clean emotional conclusion
- a thesis sentence
- a quote-like line

Prefer ending with:

- action
- physical image
- unanswered dialogue
- choice
- object
- sensory detail
- a small contradiction
- a consequence beginning

## Step 10: Do the final naturalness read

Ask:

- Does this feel observed or engineered?
- Does the character experience the scene, or does the narrator explain it?
- Does every line try to be meaningful?
- Are the strongest lines still present?
- Did the revision preserve voice?

If the revision became bland, restore selected voice and imagery.

# Plain Physical Rewrite Method

Use this method when the user asks for a humanized rewrite, AI-ism cleanup, or naturalness pass.

## 1. Read the whole passage first

Do not line-edit immediately. Understand what actually happens and what must remain unchanged.

## 2. Mark the seven plain-prose problems

Mark:

- decorative verbs
- poetic endings
- beautiful observations the character would not make
- over-explained emotion
- redundant explanatory sentences
- cushioning around hard facts
- prose that notices its own cleverness

## 3. Rewrite without changing the story

Keep the same events, order, characters, and dialogue unless asked otherwise.

Prefer simple physical wording:

- He ran.
- She looked at him.
- The water was cold.
- The door shut.

Avoid upgraded wording that only sounds better:

- He sprinted, driven by panic.
- Her eyes found his.
- The cold hit him like a wall.
- The door whispered shut.

## 4. Run a second pass against the rewrite

Check whether the rewrite introduced new decorative verbs, poetic endings, or clever after-lines.

The final version should feel like it says what happened.
