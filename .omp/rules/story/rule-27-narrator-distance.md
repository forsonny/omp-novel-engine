# Rule 27: Banned Patterns - Narrator-Distance Constructions That Signal AI Authorship

## The Rule

Certain sentence constructions appear in AI-generated prose at rates that make them identifiable as machine tics. They share a common mechanism: they insert distance between the reader and the character's direct experience by routing perception through a narrator who compares, categorizes, announces, or analyzes instead of simply perceiving. Each pattern below is banned. Not reduced. Banned. The occasional exception is noted where one exists.

---

## Pattern 1: "The Way" Comparisons

**The construction:** "X the way Y" / "the same way" / "the way you'd" / "the way a [person] would"

**Why AI produces it:** AI reaches for comparison as a shortcut to specificity. Instead of rendering what the character perceives directly, it explains the perception by analogy. The analogy feels vivid on first encounter. By the fourth or fifth instance in a chapter, the reader is being pulled out of the scene into a comparison every few paragraphs.

**Example (wrong):**
> He studied me the way I'd study a fence line before deciding which posts needed replacing.

**Example (correct):**
> He studied me. His eyes went to my hands first, then my shoulders, then my blade, measuring what could bear weight.

The correct version puts the reader inside the observation. The wrong version steps outside it to explain what kind of observation it was.

**The ban:** Zero tolerance. Do not write "X the way Y" or "the same way" or "the way you'd" under any circumstances. There is always a direct alternative.

**Exception:** Dialogue. Characters speak in comparisons naturally. A character saying "You fight the way a drunk kicks a door" is voice. The narrator writing "He fought the way a drunk kicks a door" is a crutch. The ban applies to narration only.

---

## Pattern 2: "The [Quality] of a [Person] Who [Verb]" Type-Casting

**The construction:** "the unhurried precision of someone who considered urgency beneath him" / "the quiet confidence of a man who had done this walk before" / "the expression of a person who has just decided you are something to be careful of"

**Why AI produces it:** When AI introduces a character or describes a reaction, it categorizes the person into a type rather than rendering the specific physical detail the POV character would actually see. The construction gives the reader a character profile instead of a character. It is narrator-mode at its most visible: the narrator is interpreting a person for the reader, packaging them into a labeled behavioral category.

**Example (wrong):**
> He moved with the unhurried precision of someone who considered urgency beneath him.

**Example (correct):**
> He moved slowly. Nothing in this tent was going to make him hurry.

**Example (wrong):**
> She spoke with the quiet confidence of a woman who had done this many times before.

**Example (correct):**
> She didn't raise her voice. She didn't need to.

The correct version delivers the observation and lets the reader draw the conclusion. The wrong version delivers the conclusion in place of the observation.

**The ban:** Zero tolerance in narration. Do not describe any character using "the [quality/expression/tone/manner/precision/confidence] of a [man/woman/person/someone] who [verb clause]." Find the physical detail the POV character would actually perceive and write that instead. Let the reader build the character profile from observed behavior.

**Exception:** None. This construction is always narrator interpretation disguised as character observation.

---

## Pattern 3: Negative Definition Chains

**The construction:** "Not X. Not Y. Z." / "Not angry. Not amused. Assessing." / "Not hostile. Not curious. Not anything."

**Why AI produces it:** AI hedges. When it needs to name what a character is showing, it often circles the target by first eliminating what the character is not showing. This is a machine running through its probability space out loud. The reader receives a list of rejected labels before the selected label arrives.

**Example (wrong):**
> Not angry. Not amused. Assessing the distance between what was in front of them and what was needed.

**Example (correct):**
> Measuring. Their eyes went from my hands to my blade to my face and made a judgment before I'd finished speaking.

**The ban:** Do not define characters, emotions, or states by listing what they are not. Name what the character IS doing or showing. One negation in a sentence is acceptable ("He wasn't smiling"). A chain of two or more negations used to circle toward a positive statement is banned.

**Exception:** Dialogue. Characters negate naturally in speech: "I'm not angry. I'm not even disappointed. I just don't care." This is voice. Narration that uses the same structure is a tic.

**Exception:** A single negation that creates genuine surprise. "He didn't flinch" can be correct if the reader expected the character to flinch. This is information, not hedging. The ban targets chains and lists of negation, not individual negated observations.

---

## Pattern 4: Retrospective Narrator Framing

**The construction:** "I didn't know yet that..." / "Later, I would learn..." / "though I didn't understand it at the time" / "I didn't know what that meant yet" / "which I would later discover was..."

**Why AI produces it:** AI has access to the full outline. It knows what happens next. When writing a scene, it leaks that knowledge by having the narrator reference future events or future understanding from a vantage point the character does not occupy during the scene. This creates a narrator who is visibly older and wiser than the character experiencing the moment, which splits the reader's attention between the present experience and the narrator's commentary on it.

**Example (wrong):**
> I didn't know the rules yet. I didn't know that talking to your spirit out loud was something the inner rings considered crude. Later, people would stare and whisper, and I would learn the word for what I was doing wrong.

**Example (correct):**
> I heard a voice and answered it. She spoke and I spoke back. It seemed obvious.

The correct version stays in the moment. The character does what makes sense to him. The social friction arrives when it arrives, in its own scene, through other characters' reactions. The narrator does not preview it.

**The ban:** The character is in the scene. If they do not know something, they do not reference it. They do not acknowledge the gap in their knowledge from a future position. They do not hint at what is coming. They experience what is in front of them.

**Exception:** A narrator who is explicitly telling the story from a future vantage point (a framing device where the character is recounting events years later) can use retrospective framing as a deliberate structural choice. This must be established in the book's first chapter and maintained consistently. It cannot appear sporadically.

---

## Pattern 5: Narrator Announcements of Significance

**The construction:** "Something had changed." / "I could feel it in the space around me." / "That was when I realized." / "Everything was different now." / "I understood then."

**Why AI produces it:** When a scene reaches a moment of shift or realization, AI announces the shift instead of enacting it. The narrator steps forward and tells the reader that something important just happened, rather than letting the reader feel the importance through what happens next. This is the narrator pointing at the moment and saying "look at this" instead of trusting the moment to land.

**Example (wrong):**
> Something had changed. I could feel it in the space around me, in the way people moved.

**Example (correct):**
> Two soldiers who had been walking toward me stopped. The nearer one said something to the other. They didn't come closer.

The correct version shows the change through specific behavior. The reader feels the shift because the world has shifted around the character. The wrong version announces the shift and then provides the evidence as illustration of the announcement.

**The ban:** Do not announce changes, realizations, or moments of significance. Show the change through behavior, through physical detail, through what the character does or perceives next. If the sentence begins with "Something had" or "That was when" or "I realized that" or "Everything was," cut it. Find the specific thing that changed and write that instead.

**Exception:** None. Every announcement can be replaced with the specific observation it was announcing.

---

## Pattern 6: Emotional Analysis After Observation

**The construction:** An emotional observation followed by sentences explaining the emotion's pattern, origin, or significance.

**Why AI produces it:** Rule 03 addresses emotional over-writing generally. This pattern is more specific: it is the narrator using an observation as a springboard into analysis of a character's emotional habits. The AI has noticed something about a character and wants to make sure the reader understands it, so it explains the pattern rather than trusting the observation to stand.

**Example (wrong):**
> She said it lightly. I'd noticed that about her. The moments where her warmth covered something she didn't want to look at were easy to spot once you knew the shape.

**Example (correct):**
> She said it lightly. I didn't push.

The correct version trusts the reader. "I didn't push" implies he noticed something fragile in the lightness. The reader gets it. The wrong version stops the scene to deliver the narrator's accumulated understanding of the character's emotional patterns.

**The ban:** After an emotional observation, the next sentence must be action, dialogue, or a new perception. It cannot be analysis of what was just observed. The narrator does not explain characters to the reader. Characters reveal themselves through what they do and say. One line of observation, then move.

**Exception:** Interior monologue scenes (Rule 03's 10% exception) where the emotional analysis IS the scene's event. In those scenes, the character is actively processing. The analysis is the action. Outside of those scenes, analysis after observation is banned.

---

## The Master Test

After drafting any passage, search for these constructions:

1. Any sentence containing "the way" used as a comparison
2. Any phrase matching "the [noun] of a [person] who"
3. Any sequence of two or more "Not X" negations
4. Any sentence referencing future knowledge ("I didn't know yet," "Later," "I would learn")
5. Any sentence announcing that something has changed or been realized
6. Any sentence that analyzes an emotional observation instead of moving to the next action

If any are found: cut the sentence. Rewrite using the direct alternative. There is always a direct alternative. These constructions are never necessary. They are always the narrator stepping between the reader and the character's experience.

---

## Why These Patterns Matter More Than They Appear To

Each individual instance of these patterns is minor. "The way you'd look at a stray dog" is a serviceable simile. "The unhurried precision of someone who considered urgency beneath him" is competent characterization. "I didn't know what that meant yet" is a reasonable narrative aside.

The problem is frequency. AI does not produce one of these per chapter. It produces four or five per chapter, across multiple patterns simultaneously. At that density, the patterns become the prose's texture. The reader spends more time in the narrator's comparison-space than in the character's direct perception. The voice stops being the character's voice and becomes the AI's voice, which has a specific and recognizable quality: clever, careful, slightly distant, always explaining.

The ban is not because these constructions are bad writing. It is because AI cannot use them in moderation. The only reliable way to eliminate the density problem is to eliminate the constructions entirely and force the prose into direct perception, where the character's voice lives.
