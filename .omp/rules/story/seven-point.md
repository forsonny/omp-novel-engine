# Writing a Novel with the Seven-Point Story Structure

## Executive summary

The Seven-Point Story Structure is a compact plotting framework popularized by novelist Dan Wells. In Wells’s formulation, the seven points are Hook, Plot Turn I, Pinch I, Midpoint, Pinch II, Plot Turn II, and Resolution. Its practical strength is not that it replaces every other planning method, but that it gives you a sturdy backbone for a novel: a beginning state, a transformed ending state, a midpoint that flips the protagonist from reacting to acting, and four pressure-and-transition beats that make the change believable. Wells explicitly recommends working backward from the ending, then defining the opposite or faraway starting state, then filling in the middle turns and pressure points. citeturn29view3turn16view0

For novelists, the most useful way to apply the framework is not to treat the seven points as seven isolated scenes. Wells says they are key moments rather than the only things that happen in the story, and he also says the same cycle can recur at smaller scales, including subplots and even scenes. Complementary craft guidance from K.M. Weiland is useful here: major beats function as turning points, but the story actually lives in the sections between them, where cause and effect, scene-sequel logic, and evolving character intention create momentum. citeturn29view3turn21view0turn17view16

For a novel-length workflow, the most robust process is: define premise and stakes, choose the Resolution, build the Hook as its contrast, place the Midpoint, add the two plot turns, add the two pinch points, expand the spaces between beats into scene sequences, then package those sequences into chapters. That workflow blends Wells’s reverse engineering with standard outlining practice, scene structure, and Snowflake-style expansion from premise to chapter-level detail. citeturn29view3turn18view0turn18view1turn18view5

Assumptions used in this report: “short,” “medium,” and “long” refer to 50,000, 80,000, and 120,000 words because those are the planning targets requested here, not fixed market categories. Chapter estimates assume adult-fiction chapters often land around 2,000 to 4,000 words, while still ending where their narrative purpose ends rather than at a mandated size. All chapter-zone numbers below are therefore planning aids, not rules. Confidence on chapter-zone estimates: medium. citeturn17view12turn17view13

## Definition and origin

In direct, practical terms, the Seven-Point Story Structure is an outlining system that identifies seven key structural moments that move a story from Hook to Resolution. On Writing Excuses, Wells describes the Hook as the starting state, Plot Turn I as the call to adventure, Pinch I as pressure, the Midpoint as the shift from reaction to action, Pinch II as intensified pressure, Plot Turn II as the final piece needed to win, and the Resolution as the moment the protagonist “does it” and saves the day. He also says the method is best used by starting with the Resolution and then building backward toward the Hook. citeturn16view0

A terminology warning matters here. This report uses the beat names you requested: Hook, First Plot Point, First Pinch, Midpoint, Second Pinch, Second Plot Point, and Resolution. That aligns cleanly with Wells’s Plot Turn I and Plot Turn II. However, in adjacent three-act and four-act teaching, the 75 percent gateway is often called the Third Plot Point, and some teachers reserve “Resolution” for the post-climax denouement rather than the climactic win itself. Wells’s own language places the climactic “save the day” action inside Resolution, while Weiland distinguishes between Climax and a short Resolution/afterglow that follows it. Confidence on this naming variation: high. citeturn16view0turn22view5turn31view0

On origin, the strongest directly verified claim is Wells’s own: he says he got the structure from a role-playing book and that it is also common in screenwriting. Secondary writing sources frequently identify the role-playing source as the Star Trek Roleplaying Game Narrator’s Guide, but I did not verify that book directly here. The safest phrasing is therefore that Wells popularized the framework for many novelists, adapted it from a role-playing source, and presented it as a screenwriting-adjacent story tool. Confidence on the exact upstream source beyond Wells’s own account: medium. citeturn29view3turn26view0

The model is best understood as approximate pacing rather than mechanical arithmetic. citeturn30view0turn17view0turn17view1turn17view2turn17view3turn31view0

## Beat-by-beat novel map

For novels, major beats are usually best treated as scenes or short sequences, not as single paragraph-sized events. Weiland calls the major plot points set-piece scenes or sequences, and Wells emphasizes that the seven points are key moments rather than the whole book. That distinction matters because the spaces between beats carry the try-fail cycles, subplot braiding, escalation, and causality that keep a novel from feeling skeletal. citeturn22view3turn29view3turn21view0

| Beat | Structural job | Percent anchor | Word target at 50k / 80k / 120k | Typical chapter zone if chapters average 2k-4k words | Character-arc movement | Tailored brainstorming prompts |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|---|---|
| Hook | Introduce the protagonist’s current world, a compelling question, and the first domino | 0-1% | opening pages / opening pages / opening pages | Opening scene or opening chapter | Show the flaw, wound, lie, or limitation that the ending will challenge | What is “normal” right now? What is missing or broken beneath that normal? What image, tone, or unanswered question can quietly foreshadow the ending? |
| First Plot Point | Force entry into the main conflict, often as an irreversible threshold | 25% | 12,500 / 20,000 / 30,000 | roughly ch. 3-6 / 5-10 / 8-15 | Old coping strategy stops working; external goal becomes non-optional | What happens that the protagonist cannot undo? What concrete choice or event locks them into the main story? What stake becomes impossible to ignore? |
| First Pinch | Make the antagonistic force felt and raise stakes | 37% | 18,500 / 29,600 / 44,400 | roughly ch. 5-9 / 7-15 / 11-22 | First consequences land; illusion of safety thins | How does the story demonstrate, not merely mention, the force opposing the protagonist? What can be lost now that was only theoretical before? |
| Midpoint | Deliver the central revelation or reversal that shifts reaction into action | 50% | 25,000 / 40,000 / 60,000 | roughly ch. 6-12 / 10-20 / 15-30 | Protagonist sees a truth, names the real problem, or takes ownership of the conflict | What does the protagonist finally understand at the center of the book? | Second Pinch | Tighten the vise, isolate the protagonist, and point toward the low moment | 62% | 31,000 / 49,600 / 74,400 | roughly ch. 8-16 / 12-25 / 19-37 | The cost of not changing becomes painful and immediate | What gets worse in a way the protagonist cannot shrug off? | Second Plot Point | Provide the final piece needed for the endgame, often after or inside a low moment | 75% | 37,500 / 60,000 / 90,000 | roughly ch. 10-19 / 15-30 / 22-45 | Lesson and plot knowledge fuse into a workable final plan | What key information, courage, ally, tool, sacrifice, or self-knowledge makes the finale possible? What loose ends must readers see resolved, and what final emotion should remain when they close the book? |

One of the biggest practical gains of this model is that it lets you plan both macro structure and micro execution. Wells explicitly says it can help with subplots and can even repeat in miniature inside scenes. Matched with scene-sequel structure, that means each large beat can be broken into purposeful sub-scenes: goal, conflict, and disaster, followed by reaction, dilemma, and decision. That is often the missing bridge between “I know my big beats” and “I know what tomorrow’s writing session is about.” citeturn29view0turn17view16turn21view0

## Brainstorming, planning, and outlining

A strong Seven-Point plan usually begins by answering three questions that standard outlining advice also emphasizes: what promise the story makes, what pressure the characters are under, and what is at stake. From there, build a premise, then identify the Resolution, its opposite Hook, and the Midpoint that pivots the story from one state toward the other. Wells’s reverse-order logic and modern outline guidance align well here; both aim to keep the writer focused on promises, stakes, and consequence before scene-level detail multiplies. citeturn20view2turn16view0

| Planning tool | What it contains | Advantages | Drawbacks | Best use case |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Beat sheet | One-line summary of premise, stakes, character arc, and major turning points | Fast, clarifies structure early, excellent for diagnosing missing turns | Can feel abstract; weak on chronology and logistics if used alone | Early ideation, structural check, revision triage |
| Scene list | Ordered list of scenes with POV, setting, goal, conflict, turn, and aftermath | Strong for cause-and-effect, pacing, subplot braiding, and daily drafting targets | More time-consuming; can become over-detailed before the story is stable | When you are ready to draft or repair a messy middle |
| Chapter outline | Groups one or more scenes into chapters with chapter openings, closures, and cliffhangers | Useful for reader pace, chapter hooks, and market-facing rhythm | Chapters are arbitrary relative to scenes, so chapter-first planning can distort structure | Useful late in outlining, especially for commercial pacing and multiple POVs |

Source basis: a novel outline can range from a short document to a visual card system; sticky notes or index cards are effective because scene descriptions stay short and movable; chapter length should serve purpose rather than obey a rigid rule. citeturn18view0turn18view5turn17view13

A practical Seven-Point outlining method, from high level to chapter level, looks like this:

1. Write a one-sentence premise and pressure-test it with a “what if” formulation. Then name the protagonist, the central problem or goal, the opposing force, and the stakes. citeturn20view0turn20view2turn20view5

2. Define the Resolution first. Ask what decisive action or choice ends the central conflict and what changed state you want readers to see. In Wells’s method, this instantly gives you direction for the Hook. citeturn16view0turn29view3

3. Build the Hook as contrast. If the ending proves courage, the opening should dramatize avoidance. If the ending secures belonging, the opening should dramatize alienation. This contrast is not mandatory symmetry, but it is an efficient way to generate arc. citeturn29view3turn30view0

4. Place the Midpoint before adding the pinches. Wells defines the Midpoint as the reaction-to-action turn, and Weiland treats it as the literal centerpiece of the story. If you cannot articulate what the protagonist understands or decides at 50 percent, the rest of the outline usually remains mushy. citeturn16view0turn17view2

5. Add the two plot turns, then the two pinch points. First Plot Point is the threshold into main conflict; Second Plot Point is the final key to the endgame; the pinch points reinforce antagonistic force and stakes. This gives you a stable seven-row beat sheet. citeturn16view0turn22view2turn22view5

6. Expand the spaces between beats into section goals and scene sequences. Weiland’s advice is especially valuable here: the story lives between beats. For each section, write the protagonist’s current intent, the main obstacle, the failed approach, the consequence, and the next intent. If the section contains several scenes, think in sequences, not isolated fragments. citeturn21view0

7. Package scenes into chapters only after the scene list works. Use cards, sticky notes, or a board so you can move scenes around. Then group them by rhythm: a chapter should feel like a meaningful “commercial break,” not a random word-count chunk. citeturn18view5turn17view13

A compact blank beat sheet template:

| Field | Fill in |
|---|---|
| Premise | 1 sentence |
| Protagonist | name, misbelief, longing, fear |
| External goal | what they want |
| Stakes | what is lost if they fail |
| Hook | opening state and question |
| First Plot Point | threshold event |
| First Pinch | first strong pressure beat |
| Midpoint | revelation and proactive turn |
| Second Pinch | harsher pressure or loss |
| Second Plot Point | final key, truth, ally, or plan |
| Resolution | climactic action and new normal |
| Subplots to braid | romance, friendship, mystery, etc. |

A practical scene template, built from scene-sequel logic:

| Scene field | What to enter |
|---|---|
| Scene ID and chapter | e.g., S12, Ch. 5 |
| POV and time | whose head, when |
| Structural section | between which beats this scene belongs |
| Scene goal | what the POV character is trying to do now |
| Conflict | what blocks them |
| Turn or disaster | what changes, fails, or worsens |
| Sequel reaction | emotional or tactical response |
| Decision | what the character chooses next |

The scene template above is a synthesis of the classic goal-conflict-disaster and reaction-dilemma-decision model, which is one of the cleanest ways to turn a high-level beat map into draftable prose. citeturn17view16turn21view0

## Drafting, revision, and troubleshooting

A rough draft is supposed to be rough. Its job is to get the story bones onto the page, reveal structural holes, and prove whether the character trajectory actually works in motion. That matters with Seven-Point planning because a beat sheet is only a hypothesis until scenes exist. The draft tests whether each beat earns the next one. citeturn18view2

If you reserve the last 2 weeks of a 12-week cycle for revision, your average drafting pace across the first 10 weeks is straightforward math: 5,000 words per week for a 50,000-word draft, 8,000 words per week for an 80,000-word draft, and 12,000 words per week for a 120,000-word draft. Across 5 drafting days per week, that becomes roughly 1,000, 1,600, and 2,400 words per day. Those are realistic only if the outline is serviceable and the daily target is tied to scenes and sections, not wishful thinking.

| Week | Structural focus | Draft target at 50k | Draft target at 80k | Draft target at 120k | Revision checkpoint |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---|
| 1 | Hook, setup, first hooks, early causality | 4,000 | 6,400 | 9,600 | Does the opening pose a real story question? |
| 2 | Finish Act I, reach First Plot Point | 4,000 | 6,400 | 9,600 | Is there a clear door of no return by the end of this week’s pages? |
| 3 | Early Act II, first responses to the threshold | 5,000 | 8,000 | 12,000 | Are scenes showing adaptation, not wheel-spinning? |
| 4 | Build toward First Pinch | 5,000 | 8,000 | 12,000 | Has the antagonistic force become concrete? |
| 5 | Drive to Midpoint | 6,000 | 9,600 | 14,400 | What truth or reversal will flip the protagonist into action? |
| 6 | Write the Midpoint and immediate aftermath | 6,000 | 9,600 | 14,400 | Have tactics changed on the page, not just in notes? |
| 7 | Post-midpoint action, narrowing options | 5,000 | 8,000 | 12,000 | Is the second half more proactive than the first? |
| 8 | Reach and exploit Second Pinch | 5,000 | 8,000 | 12,000 | Has the cost deepened, not merely repeated? |
| 9 | Move into the Act III gateway, write Second Plot Point | 5,000 | 8,000 | 12,000 | Does the protagonist gain the final key and make a credible final plan? |
| 10 | Draft finale, climactic action, and brief denouement | 5,000 | 8,000 | 12,000 | Does the ending dramatize change and answer the main promise? |
| 11 | Structural revision pass | 0 new words required | 0 new words required | 0 new words required | Rebuild weak beats, move scenes, cut dead sections |
| 12 | Scene-level and prose-level revision | 0 new words required | 0 new words required | 0 new words required | Sharpen openings, exits, transitions, and emotional continuity |

This schedule reflects three linked craft realities: rough drafts test story bones, revision strengthens novels after “The End,” and structure, character, and stakes must all hold together for the manuscript to read like a real story rather than arranged incidents. citeturn18view2turn18view3turn18view4

A beat-linked revision checklist is often more effective than a generic reread:

| Beat | Diagnostic question | Typical failure mode | Repair move |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hook | Does the first chapter introduce a story-worthy problem, hook readers, establish the rules, and forecast where this story is headed? | Openings that are atmospheric but inert | Add a sharper characteristic moment, clearer tension, and a future-facing question |
| First Plot Point | Is there an irreversible threshold near 25 percent? | The book “starts” too late or too slowly | Strengthen the commitment, collapse pre-threshold wandering, or move the threshold earlier |
| First Pinch | Does the antagonistic force become tangible? | Middle feels safe or vague | Replace exposition with a concrete pressure event and visible stakes |
| Midpoint | Does the protagonist learn or decide something that changes tactics? | Midpoint is just a cool event | Add revelation, self-recognition, or a new operational plan |
| Second Pinch | Does the story deepen rather than repeat pressure? | Act II feels repetitive | Increase cost, isolate the protagonist, or threaten something newly precious |
| Second Plot Point | Is there truly a final key that makes the climax possible? | Finale feels lucky or unprepared | Seed and then reveal the missing knowledge, ally, courage, or cost |
| Resolution | Does the ending show decisive conflict resolution and a changed normal? | Ending summarizes instead of dramatizing | Put proof of change into action, then keep denouement short and emotionally precise |

The Hook checklist draws on opening-scene goals from Writer’s Digest, while the structural timing and function of the later beats follow Wells and Weiland. citeturn17view15turn16view0turn17view0turn17view1turn17view2turn22view0turn31view0

The most common global troubleshooting issues are mechanical use of percentages, confusing chapters with beats, and forgetting that subplots also require progression. Wells specifically warns that the seven points are not the only things in the story, and Weiland explicitly argues that momentum comes from the sections between plot points. If a novel feels “correct” but dead, the fix is usually not another beat, but stronger scene causality, sharper intentions, and more meaningful consequences in the spaces between beats. citeturn29view3turn21view0

## Worked examples

The two examples below are original demonstration synopses created for this report. They are intentionally brief so you can see the Seven-Point logic without the noise of a full synopsis.

### Example one

**The Ash Atlas** is an adult fantasy novel. Elara Venn repairs damaged maps in a quiet archive and avoids risk because her father vanished on an expedition years ago. When her brother disappears chasing a map that changes every dawn, she is forced onto the road after him. She discovers that a hidden city survives by burning names, borders, and memories out of the world itself. To save her brother, and to keep whole towns from being erased from history, Elara must enter a living atlas that turns memory into geography and choose whether preserving the past is worth losing the person she has been.

| Beat | Filled beat |
|---|---|
| Hook | Elara restores ruined maps in the archive and refuses all fieldwork, silently ashamed that she let her father go alone years ago |
| First Plot Point | Her brother steals a self-redrawing map and vanishes; Elara leaves the archive with smugglers to follow the route |
| First Pinch | Atlas hunters destroy a waystation and erase every local record of it, proving the enemy can literally remove places from memory |
| Midpoint | Elara learns the hidden city feeds on erased histories and realizes her brother is part of a much larger purge; she stops chasing rescue alone and commits to stopping the city itself |
| Second Pinch | She finds her brother alive but memory-altered and loyal to the city; the archive she hoped to save is burned |
| Second Plot Point | In her father’s notes, Elara discovers that places can be restored if their true names are spoken by someone who still remembers them intact |
| Resolution | Elara enters the living atlas, restores the lost names during the final confrontation, breaks the city’s power, and returns with her brother’s memories slowly recovering |

### Example two

**Signal at Mile Forty-Three** is a contemporary suspense novel. Mara Sloane restores damaged audio for a city archive and still hates herself for once hearing evidence of corruption and staying silent. On an overnight mountain train, a terrified stranger slips her a recorder and vanishes at a tunnel stop. The device contains fragments of proof that a rail contractor plans a sabotage event to bury a bribery trail. Trapped among passengers she cannot trust, Mara must decode the damaged audio, survive the contractor’s security team, and decide whether she will finally speak in public when silence would be safer.

| Beat | Filled beat |
|---|---|
| Hook | Mara lives quietly inside old recordings and avoids career risk after previously failing to report corruption she overheard |
| First Plot Point | A dying whistleblower hands her a recorder on the night train, then disappears, making Mara the unwilling carrier of evidence |
| First Pinch | Rail security frames her for theft and searches the train, while the recorder reveals the contractor’s voice discussing a timed “incident” |
| Midpoint | Mara realizes the planned sabotage will happen before dawn and that the train engineer she distrusted is actually trying to stop it; she shifts from hiding the recorder to actively exposing the plot |
| Second Pinch | The engineer is arrested, Mara loses phone access, and the main audio file is wiped, leaving only distorted fragments |
| Second Plot Point | Mara discovers the missing proof is encoded in station-chime frequencies and can be decoded through the train’s PA hardware |
| Resolution | She hijacks the intercom, reconstructs the signal live, exposes the contractor, helps stop the sabotage, and publicly admits her earlier silence |

These examples illustrate a practical truth about the Seven-Point model: the framework is especially powerful when the Hook dramatizes the protagonist’s pre-change identity, the Midpoint produces a genuine tactical pivot, and the climax proves change through action rather than internal monologue alone. That is why the grid works so well for both external plots and character arcs. citeturn16view0turn18view4
